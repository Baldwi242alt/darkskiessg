My Plan Your Plan
Why this matters to me

The night I saw the stars

Having lived in Singapore my entire life, I grew accustomed to a night sky shaped by constant artificial lighting. From my home in Bishan, the city's amber glow often obscures the stars, making true darkness increasingly rare in an urban environment.

During a family trip to Cameron Highlands in 2025, I witnessed a completely different night sky — one filled with countless visible stars, with the Milky Way stretching clearly across the horizon. The contrast was striking. Returning to Singapore made me realise how much excessive nighttime lighting has gradually taken away from our connection with the natural world.

That experience transformed light pollution from a distant environmental issue into something deeply personal. It reinforced my belief that sustainable urban lighting is not only about reducing energy consumption, but also about protecting biodiversity, improving human well-being, and preserving the beauty of the night sky for future generations.

My commitment

A 3-month action timeline

Those are specific, measurable steps I have committed to completing between May and July 2026. Each build on the last.

M1

MONTH 1

May 2026

  • Audit every light in my home
  • Replace cool-white bulbs (6500K) with warm-white LEDs (2700K)
  • Install motion-sensor switches on corridor lights
  • Download light pollution Map & Loss of the Night apps
M2

MONTH 2

June 2026

  • Present day sky awareness to my Computing Club
  • Collect neighbour signatures for HDB TC petition
  • Write formal letter to Bishan Town Council requesting shielded corridor fixtures
  • Participate in Globe at Night June star count
M3

MONTH 3

July 2026

  • Record sky brightness readings using Loss of the Night app
  • Compare before and after Bortle scale ratings
  • Plan a night visit to Pulau Ubin to observe relative darkness
  • Share findings and update this website

How You can make a difference

You do not need to be an astronomer or an activist. Every action — however small — adds up across a city.

1

Switch One bulb

Replace one cool-white bulb with a warm-white LED (2700 K). It costs under $5 and immediately reduces the harmful blue-spectrum glow.

2

Join Globe at Night

Count how many stars you can see, then submit your data at globeatnight.org↗. You become part of a worldwide citizen science database.

3

Visit Pulau Ubin

Go after dark. Singapore’s last relatively dark sky refuge is just one bumboat ride from Changi Point Ferry Terminal. Look up — you may see a handful of stars.

4

Spread Awareness

Share darksky.org↗ or this website with your neighbours, condo management, or school sustainability club.
